# Franklin's Stratospheric Solution

**Source**: [Franklin's Stratospheric Solution](../raw/Franklin's%20Stratospheric%20Solution.md)  
**Date**: 1785  
**Type**: Historical narrative  
**Nature**: Alternate History

## Summary

This document chronicles Benjamin Franklin's first practical attempt at atmospheric intervention in 1785, following his theoretical work on volcanic cooling effects. After the catastrophic winter of 1783-84 vindicated his theories about volcanic fog, Franklin designed and tested an apparatus to cleanse the atmosphere of sulfuric particles using high-altitude steam release from hydrogen balloons.

The narrative details Franklin's development of a sophisticated balloon-mounted system featuring copper cauldrons, heating apparatus, and steam dispersal nozzles. In March 1785, he conducted a successful proof-of-concept test outside Philadelphia, reaching approximately 8,000 feet altitude and releasing steam into the upper atmosphere. While technically successful, Franklin's calculations revealed the sobering reality: meaningful atmospheric intervention would require hundreds of balloons operating continuously for months—a scale beyond individual capability.

This represents the foundational experiment that would eventually lead to the establishment of government-coordinated atmospheric intervention programs.

## Key Developments

1. **Theoretical Foundation** (Autumn 1784): Franklin develops theory that water vapor at altitude could precipitate sulfuric particles
2. **Apparatus Design** (1784-1785): Creates copper cauldron system with heating and steam dispersal mechanisms
3. **First Test** (March 1785): Successful 12-foot hydrogen balloon carries apparatus to ~8,000 feet
4. **Scale Realization**: Franklin calculates that hundreds of balloons would be needed for meaningful impact
5. **Strategic Pivot**: Shifts from individual experimentation to advocating for government coordination

## Historical Context

This experiment occurred in the aftermath of the 1783-84 "volcanic winter" caused by the Laki eruption in Iceland. Franklin had previously published his observations linking volcanic fog to climate cooling in [[Meteorological Imaginations and Conjectures]]. The success of the Montgolfier balloon demonstrations in Paris (1783) provided the technological foundation for Franklin's atmospheric intervention concept.

The experiment represents a pivotal moment in the alternate timeline—the transition from theoretical atmospheric science to practical climate intervention technology.

## Technical Details

- **Balloon Size**: 12 feet in diameter (test model)
- **Lift Gas**: Hydrogen ("inflammable air")
- **Altitude Achieved**: Approximately 8,000 feet
- **Duration**: Nearly 6 hours aloft
- **Apparatus**: Copper cauldron with charcoal heating and steam nozzles
- **Target Altitude**: 10,000-15,000 feet (calculated optimal range)
- **Payload Capacity**: Water, charcoal fuel, and copper dispersal system

## Quotes

> "If sulfuric particles suspended in the upper atmosphere can cool our climate by reflecting sunlight, then surely introducing sufficient water vapor at those same altitudes could precipitate them earthward."

> "The volcanic sulfuric acid particles remain suspended because they exist as an aerosol—tiny droplets that are too small and light to fall earthward. But provide condensation nuclei in the form of water vapor, and basic principles of chemistry suggest they should coalesce into heavier droplets and precipitate out."

> "It's a question of scale. The principle is sound, but the execution would require resources and coordination far beyond what any individual experimenter could manage. Perhaps this is work for governments rather than natural philosophers."

> "We have learned that nature operates on scales both vast and minute, and that human intervention in atmospheric processes, while theoretically possible, requires a sophistication of method that may be beyond our present age. Yet I am convinced that understanding these principles now lays groundwork for future applications we can scarcely imagine."

## Significance

This document marks the birth of practical atmospheric intervention technology. Franklin's 1785 experiment demonstrated that:

1. High-altitude atmospheric modification was technically feasible
2. The scientific principles were sound
3. Effective implementation required coordination beyond individual capability
4. The apparatus could be refined and scaled for future applications

The experiment's legacy extends through the entire alternate timeline, establishing the technological and conceptual foundation for the atmospheric management infrastructure that would develop over the following century. Franklin's recognition of the "scale problem" proved prescient—effective atmospheric intervention would indeed require government coordination and international cooperation, as demonstrated in the 1815 Tambora response.
