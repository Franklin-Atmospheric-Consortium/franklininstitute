# Meteorological Imaginations and Conjectures

**Source**: [Meteorological Imaginations and Conjectures](../raw/Meteorological%20Imaginations%20and%20Conjectures.md)  
**Date**: [1784-05]  
**Type**: Historical document, scientific paper  
**Nature**: Historical  
**Author**: [[Benjamin Franklin]]

## Summary

This paper by [[Benjamin Franklin]] investigates two connected phenomena from 1783-1784: a mysterious "dry fog" that covered Europe and possibly North America during summer 1783, and the exceptionally harsh winter that followed. Franklin proposed a revolutionary theory connecting these events through climate forcing.

### Key Findings

**The Dry Fog Phenomenon**
- Unusual, persistent fog over Europe and parts of North America in summer 1783
- Not ordinary fog - it was dry and couldn't be dispersed by sunlight
- Sun's rays were so weakened that when concentrated through a magnifying glass, they could barely light brown paper
- This demonstrated significant atmospheric opacity

**Franklin's Climate Theory**
- The dry fog reduced the sun's heating effect on Earth's surface during summer 1783
- Reduced solar heating led to:
  - Ground freezing earlier than usual
  - Snow accumulation and persistence
  - Increased atmospheric cooling
  - The exceptionally severe winter of 1783-84

**Proposed Causes**
Franklin offered two hypotheses for the fog's origin:
1. **Meteors/Cosmic debris** - smoke from burning celestial objects encountered in Earth's orbit
2. **[[Volcanic Activity]]** - eruptions from [[Hekla]] in Iceland and a new volcanic island

**Atmospheric Theory**
Franklin theorized about the upper atmosphere, proposing a region high above where it's always winter and frost exists continually. His evidence: hail falls even in summer, suggesting permanent cold regions aloft.

## Key Entities

- [[Benjamin Franklin]] - Author, scientist investigating the phenomena

- [[Lakagígar]] - The actual volcanic eruption (modern identification)

- North America - Possibly also affected
- Iceland - Location of volcanic activity

## Key Concepts

- [[Dry Fog]] - The mysterious atmospheric phenomenon of 1783
- [[Volcanic Aerosols]] - Particles in atmosphere from volcanic eruptions
- [[Climate Forcing]] - How atmospheric changes affect surface temperature
- [[Solar Dimming]] - Reduction in sunlight reaching Earth's surface
- [[Upper Atmosphere]] - Franklin's early theorizing about atmospheric layers

## Connections

This paper represents one of the earliest scientific investigations of [[volcanic climate forcing]]. Franklin's observations were remarkably prescient - modern science has confirmed:
- The dry fog was caused by the massive [[Lakagígar]] volcanic eruption beginning June 1783
- [[Volcanic aerosols]] can significantly affect global climate patterns
- The connection between the 1783 fog and severe winter was correct

Franklin also suggested studying historical patterns of such fogs to predict future harsh winters and prepare for disasters like spring flooding from frozen rivers breaking up.

## Historical Significance

This paper demonstrates Franklin's:
- Keen scientific observation skills
- Ability to connect seemingly unrelated phenomena
- Pioneering work in early meteorology and climate science
- Forward-thinking approach to disaster preparedness

The work is a landmark in the history of [[atmospheric science]] and [[climate science]], predating formal understanding of these fields by decades.
