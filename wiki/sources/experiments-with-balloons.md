# Experiments with Balloons

**Source**: [Experiments with Balloons](../raw/Experiments%20with%20Balloons.md)  
**Date**: [1783-12]  
**Type**: Historical document, letters  
**Nature**: Historical  
**Author**: [[Benjamin Franklin]]

## Summary

Two letters from [[Benjamin Franklin]] written in France in December 1783 and January 1784, documenting the early balloon experiments that marked humanity's first successful flights. Franklin witnessed the historic flight of [[Charles and Robert]] and reflected on the profound implications of this new technology for human affairs, including warfare and exploration.

### Key Events Documented

**The Charles & Robert Flight (December 1, 1783)**
- Historic manned balloon flight from the Tuileries gardens in Paris
- Filled with [[inflammable air]] (hydrogen), requiring 2-3 days of constant labor
- Crew: [[Professor Charles]] (experimental philosophy) and one of the [[Robert brothers]] (constructors)
- Reached height of 300-400 toises (~600-800 meters)
- Traveled nearly seven leagues (~35 km) to L'Isle Adam before sunset
- Demonstrated controlled flight: ascending by discharging sand ballast, descending by releasing gas through a valve

**Public Spectacle**
- "Never before was a philosophical experiment so magnificently attended"
- All of Paris turned out - on quays, bridges, fields, streets, windows, rooftops
- Inhabitants from surrounding towns and villages attended
- Guns fired to signal departure
- Spectators followed the balloon until it appeared "no bigger than a walnut"

### Technical Details

**Two Types of Balloons**

1. **[[Montgolfier Balloon]]** (hot air)
   - Open at bottom
   - Straw constantly burned to maintain lift
   - Air rarified by heat is only twice as light as common air
   - Quicker and cheaper to fill
   - Must be much bigger to carry same weight

2. **[[Charles Balloon]]** (hydrogen)
   - Filled with [[inflammable air]] (hydrogen)
   - Inflammable air is ten times lighter than common air
   - Expensive and tedious filling (2-3 days)
   - Had a valve (*soupape*) at top to release gas for descent
   - Sand ballast for controlled ascent
   - Net thrown over balloon, fixed to hoop around middle, car hung from hoop

**Cost Innovation**
[[M. de Morveau]], a chemist at Dijon, discovered inflammable air from sea coal costing only 1/25th the price of the oil of vitriol (sulfuric acid) method.

## Franklin's Visionary Insights

**Military Implications**
Franklin immediately grasped the strategic significance:
> "Five thousand balloons, capable of raising two men each, could not cost more than five ships of the line; and where is the prince who can afford so to cover his country with troops for its defence, as that ten thousand men descending from the clouds might not in many places do an infinite deal of mischief, before a force could be brought together to repel them?"

He suggested this might convince "sovereigns of the folly of wars" since no dominion could be adequately guarded.

**Atmospheric Science**
Observation that a small test balloon at Vincennes fell in a contrary direction showed "mounting higher it met with a current of air in a contrary direction, an observation that may be of use to future aerial voyagers" - early recognition of wind shear and atmospheric layers.

**Public Safety Concerns**
Franklin warned about the dangers of failed experiments, noting a recent incident in Bordeaux where an unsuccessful balloon launch led to the populace pulling down the experimenter's house and nearly killing him. He emphasized the seriousness of "drawing out from their affairs all the inhabitants of a great city."

## Key Concepts

- [[Balloon Flight]] - First manned flights in human history
- [[Inflammable Air]] - Hydrogen gas, ten times lighter than air
- [[Hot Air Balloon]] - Montgolfier design using heated air

- [[Military Aviation]] - Franklin's prescient vision of aerial warfare

## Connections

These experiments occurred simultaneously with Franklin's investigation of the [[dry fog]] phenomenon documented in [[Meteorological Imaginations and Conjectures]]. The balloon technology provided humanity's first means of directly exploring the upper atmosphere that Franklin theorized about.

Franklin's letters show the intersection of:
- Scientific curiosity and rigorous observation
- Public spectacle and mass engagement with science
- Immediate recognition of military and strategic implications
- Understanding of atmospheric science (wind layers, air density)

## Historical Significance

This document captures:
- The birth of human flight
- One of history's most attended scientific experiments
- Early understanding of atmospheric physics
- Prescient vision of aerial warfare (predating military aviation by over a century)
- The Enlightenment spirit of public science

Franklin's role as witness and commentator connects American and European scientific communities during a pivotal moment in technological history.
