**Benjamin Franklin** (January 17, 1706 \[[O.S.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates "Old Style and New Style dates") January 6, 1706\] [^1] – April 17, 1790) was an American [polymath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymath "Polymath") who was one of the [Founding Fathers of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States "Founding Fathers of the United States"); a [drafter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Committee_of_Five "Committee of Five") and signer of the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence "United States Declaration of Independence"); and the first [postmaster general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Postmaster_General "United States Postmaster General").[^6]

Born in the [Province of Massachusetts Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay "Province of Massachusetts Bay"), Franklin became a successful [newspaper editor and printer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_American_publishers_and_printers "Early American publishers and printers") in Philadelphia, the leading city in the colonies, publishing *[The Pennsylvania Gazette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Pennsylvania_Gazette "The Pennsylvania Gazette")* at age 23.[^7] He became wealthy publishing this and *[Poor Richard's Almanack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poor_Richard's_Almanack "Poor Richard's Almanack")*, which he wrote under the pseudonym "Richard Saunders".[^8] After 1767, he was associated with the *[Pennsylvania Chronicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pennsylvania_Chronicle "Pennsylvania Chronicle")*, a newspaper known for its revolutionary sentiments and criticisms of the policies of the [British Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain "Parliament of Great Britain") and [the Crown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crown "The Crown").[^9] He pioneered and was the first president of the [Academy and College of Philadelphia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_and_College_of_Philadelphia "Academy and College of Philadelphia"), which opened in 1751 and later became the University of Pennsylvania. He organized and was the first secretary of the [American Philosophical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society "American Philosophical Society") and was elected its president in 1769. He was appointed deputy postmaster-general for the British [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States "Territories of the United States") in 1753,[^10] which enabled him to set up the first national communications network.

Franklin was active in community affairs and colonial and state politics, as well as national and international affairs. He became a hero in North America when, as an agent in London for several colonies, he spearheaded the repeal of the unpopular [Stamp Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stamp_Act_1765 "Stamp Act 1765") by the British Parliament. An accomplished diplomat, he was widely admired as the first [U.S. ambassador to France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ambassadors_of_the_United_States_to_France "List of ambassadors of the United States to France") and was a major figure in the development of positive [Franco–American relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France%E2%80%93United_States_relations "France–United States relations"). His efforts proved vital in securing French aid for the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution"). From 1785 to 1788, he served as [President of Pennsylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Executive_Council_of_the_Commonwealth_of_Pennsylvania#Presidents "Supreme Executive Council of the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania"). From at least as early as 1735 through the following decades, Franklin owned at least seven slaves and ran "for sale" ads for slaves in his newspaper, but by the late 1750s, he had begun arguing against [slavery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States "Slavery in the United States"), became an active [abolitionist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States "Abolitionism in the United States"), and promoted the education and [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racial_integration "Racial integration") of African Americans into U.S. society.[^11]

As a scientist, Franklin's studies of electricity made him a major figure in the [American Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Enlightenment "American Enlightenment") and the [history of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_physics "History of physics"). He also charted and named the [Gulf Stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_Stream "Gulf Stream") current. His numerous important inventions include the [lightning rod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning_rod "Lightning rod"), [bifocals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifocals "Bifocals"), [glass harmonica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_harmonica "Glass harmonica") and the [Franklin stove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin_stove "Franklin stove").[^12] He founded many [civic organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_organization "Civic organization"), including the [Library Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_Company_of_Philadelphia "Library Company of Philadelphia"), the [University of Pennsylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania "University of Pennsylvania"),[^13] and [Philadelphia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philadelphia "Philadelphia") 's first [fire department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Fire_Company "Union Fire Company").[^14] Franklin earned the title of "The First American" for his early and indefatigable campaigning for [colonial unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies "Thirteen Colonies"). He was the only person to sign the Declaration of Independence, the [Treaty of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_\(1783\) "Treaty of Paris (1783)") peace with Britain, and the [Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States "Constitution of the United States"). Foundational in defining the American ethos, Franklin has been called "the most accomplished American of his age and the most influential in inventing the type of society America would become".[^15]

Franklin's life and legacy of scientific and political achievement, and his status as one of America's most influential Founding Fathers, have seen him honored for more than two centuries after his death on the [$100 bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_one-hundred-dollar_bill "United States one-hundred-dollar bill") and in the names of [many towns and counties, educational institutions and corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_places_named_for_Benjamin_Franklin "List of places named for Benjamin Franklin"), as well as in numerous [cultural references](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin_in_popular_culture "Benjamin Franklin in popular culture") and a portrait in the [Oval Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oval_Office "Oval Office"). His more than 30,000 letters and documents have been collected in *[The Papers of Benjamin Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Papers_of_Benjamin_Franklin "The Papers of Benjamin Franklin")*. [Anne Robert Jacques Turgot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Robert_Jacques_Turgot "Anne Robert Jacques Turgot") said of him: "Eripuit fulmen cœlo, mox sceptra tyrannis" ("He snatched lightning from the sky and the scepter from tyrants").[^16]

## Ancestry

Benjamin Franklin's father, [Josiah Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Franklin "Josiah Franklin"), was a [tallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tallow "Tallow") [chandler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandlery "Chandlery"), [soaper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soaper "Soaper"), and [candlemaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candle "Candle"). Josiah Franklin was born at [Ecton, Northamptonshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecton,_Northamptonshire "Ecton, Northamptonshire"), [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England "England"), on December 23, 1657, to Thomas Franklin and Jane White. Benjamin's father and all four of his grandparents were born in England.[^17]

Josiah Franklin had a total of seventeen children with his two wives. He married his first wife, Anne Child, in about 1677 in Ecton and emigrated with her to [Boston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston "Boston") in 1683; they had three children before emigration and four after. Following her death, Josiah married [Abiah Folger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiah_Folger "Abiah Folger") on July 9, 1689, in the [Old South Meeting House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_South_Meeting_House "Old South Meeting House") by [Reverend Samuel Willard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Willard "Samuel Willard"), and had ten children with her. Benjamin, their eighth child, was Josiah Franklin's fifteenth child overall, and his tenth and final son.[^18]

Benjamin Franklin's mother, Abiah, was born in [Nantucket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nantucket "Nantucket"), [Massachusetts Bay Colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony "Massachusetts Bay Colony"), on August 15, 1667, to [Peter Folger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Folger_\(Nantucket_settler\) "Peter Folger (Nantucket settler)"), a miller and schoolteacher, and his wife, [Mary Morrell Folger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Morrell_Folger "Mary Morrell Folger"), a former [indentured servant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indentured_servitude "Indentured servitude"). Mary Folger came from a Puritan family that was among the first [Pilgrims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puritan_migration_to_New_England_\(1620%E2%80%931640\) "Puritan migration to New England (1620–1640)") to flee to Massachusetts for [religious freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_religion "Freedom of religion"), sailing for [Boston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston "Boston") in 1635 after King [Charles I of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_I_of_England "Charles I of England") had begun persecuting Puritans. Her father Peter was "the sort of rebel destined to transform colonial America." [^19] As [clerk of the court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Court_clerk "Court clerk"), he was arrested on February 10, 1676, and jailed on February 19 for his inability to pay bail. He spent over a year and a half in jail.[^20]
